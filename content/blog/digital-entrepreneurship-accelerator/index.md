So there I was, scrolling through Erasmus+ opportunities, when I spotted another project in Bulgaria. Did I hesitate? Not even for a second. I'd been there before and had a great time, plus the prices don't hurt when you're a student.

## Bulgaria: The Sequel

Let me back up a bit. My first Bulgarian adventure was in Sofia, and it was kind of accidental. Me and some friends found these ridiculously cheap flights from Bari, the kind where you wonder if they forgot to add a zero to the price. We went for a weekend, expecting nothing special, and ended up having an absolute blast. The city was buzzing, the people were cool, and as Italians, we felt like millionaires with our euros. So when I saw this training course was happening in Bulgaria again, I was like "sign me up, I'm going back!"

Plot twist though: this time we weren't going to Sofia. Nope, we were heading to Bansko, up in the mountains. I had zero idea what to expect, but I was totally game for it.

## Mountain Town Vibes

This time I actually got to know the country through local people. During my Sofia trip, I was basically just a tourist doing tourist things. In Bansko, locals showed us around, told us stories, took us to spots we'd never have found on our own. It's a completely different experience when someone who actually lives there is your guide instead of Google Maps.

## Let's Get Down to Business

Okay, so what did we actually do for a week? If you look at that schedule, it might seem like a lot of corporate buzzwords and workshop jargon, but it was actually pretty engaging. They managed to make learning about entrepreneurship not boring, which is honestly an achievement.

We kicked things off with team building and setting objectives, which I know sounds like the kind of thing that makes you want to roll your eyes, but it actually helped us all relax and figure out what we wanted to get out of this whole thing.

Then came the practical stuff. We had sessions on managing businesses, learned about tech start-ups, and actually visited real digital companies to see how they operate. The Wordpress workshops were surprisingly useful because we weren't just watching someone click around, we were actually building stuff ourselves. Turns out it's way easier to remember things when you're the one doing them. Who knew?

One thing that really stuck with me was the Business Model Canvas workshop. Basically, it's this framework where you map out your entire business idea on one page. Sounds simple, maybe even a bit silly, but it really helps you see if your "genius idea" actually makes sense or if it's just something that sounded good at 2am. Spoiler alert: most 2am ideas don't survive the canvas test.

We also did SWOT analysis, which is fancy talk for listing out what's good about your idea, what sucks, what opportunities exist, and what might destroy you. It's like giving your business idea a reality check, and honestly, we all needed that.

There were these random cool sessions too, like "QR me!" where we played around with QR codes and digital tools (remember when QR codes seemed futuristic?), and "NGO mingles" where we practiced networking without it feeling super awkward and forced.

The crowdfunding session blew my mind a bit. I always thought you needed rich investors or a bank loan to start something, but nope, apparently you can just convince random people on the internet to give you money if your idea is good enough. The internet is weird and wonderful.

## The Post Office (Yes, Really)

Throughout the whole project, we had this thing called the Post Office. Everyone made their own little mailbox out of paper (some people got really creative with theirs), and we stuck them all on a wall with our names on them. Anytime during the week, if you wanted to tell someone something, leave them a note, share a thought, or just say something nice, you could write it down and drop it in their mailbox.

I loved this. Sometimes you think of something you want to tell someone, but the moment passes, or it feels awkward to bring it up randomly. With the Post Office, you could just write it down whenever the thought hit you. I'd check my mailbox every day, and finding a new note in there always made me smile.

It was such a simple idea, but it created this ongoing conversation between all of us that ran parallel to everything else we were doing. Plus, it gave us these little keepsakes, actual tangible things we could take home. In a world where everything's digital, getting a handwritten note from someone feels kind of special.

## Food, Glorious Food

Speaking of food, Bulgarian cuisine is no joke. It's hearty, it's tasty, and there's a lot of it. We're talking about serious comfort food here. And when you mix that with Italian dishes, Spanish tapas, whatever everyone else brought, you get this amazing culinary chaos that somehow just works.

I think you learn more about people over dinner than you do in any workshop, to be honest. Someone's trying to explain their traditional dish, someone else is making fun of how they pronounce something, everyone's laughing, sharing, stealing bites off each other's plates. That's when the real connections happen.

The Bulgarians we met were super welcoming, just like in Sofia. They have this way of making you feel like you've been friends forever, even when you just met them five minutes ago.

## So What Did I Actually Learn?

By the time we wrapped up, I genuinely felt like I had some useful tools in my pocket. We weren't just talking about entrepreneurship in some abstract, theoretical way. We learned actual frameworks, played with real digital tools, pitched business ideas (some terrible, some not so terrible), and got honest feedback from people who actually know their stuff.

But honestly? The stuff I learned about business and entrepreneurship was only part of it. The feedback sessions, the Post Office, the random conversations, the shared meals, all of that taught me just as much about communication, collaboration, and understanding people from different backgrounds.

The whole mix of workshops, real business visits, hands-on practice, evening chats, and cultural mashups made it way more than just a training course. It was one of those experiences where Monday you show up not knowing anyone, and by Friday you're making plans to visit each other's countries and stay in touch. And some of us actually did keep in touch, which is pretty rare for these things.

## The Paper on Your Back Thing

One of the coolest things we did was this feedback activity during our final reflection. Picture this: everyone had a piece of paper taped to their back, and we just wandered around the room while people stopped us and wrote things on our backs. You had no idea what people were writing until the end, which made it kind of exciting and terrifying at the same time.

People wrote what they thought about you, suggestions for improvement, encouraging words, random observations. It felt weird at first, having someone scribbling on your back with a pen, but the feedback I got was surprisingly touching. Some of it was funny, some of it was really thoughtful, and all of it was honest in a way that people sometimes aren't when they're face to face with you.

I still have some of those notes, and reading them again brings back all these memories. There's something special about having physical proof that people noticed you, appreciated you, or thought you had potential. Way better than a LinkedIn recommendation, if you ask me.

## Would I Do It Again?

In a heartbeat. Mountains, snow in summer, practical skills that don't put you to sleep, awesome people from all over Europe, incredible food, handwritten notes that I still have tucked away somewhere, and prices that make Italian wallets very happy. What's not to love?

If you ever see an Erasmus+ project that looks even remotely interesting, just go. Don't overthink it. You'll learn stuff, meet people who become actual friends, eat way too much, probably embarrass yourself at least once (I definitely did), walk around with paper taped to your back, check a DIY mailbox for notes like you're in elementary school again, and come back with stories and skills that stick with you.

Bulgaria, Bansko, this whole Digital Entrepreneurship Accelerator thing? Totally worth it. 10/10 would entrepreneur again.
